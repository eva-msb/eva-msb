I study how artificial intelligence shapes human judgment and decision-making. My work examines how AI systems impact empathy, responsibility, and moral judgment, both in everyday decisions and in high-stakes institutional contexts. By combining empirical studies with broader social-philosophical analysis, I investigate how AI reshapes our capacities for care, civic responsibility, and ethical decision-making and how these shifts can inform design and governance of AI systems in society.

Conceptually, my research bridges cognitive-affective models of decision-making with sociotechnical perspectives, aiming to better understand how micro-level psychological shifts scale up to influence broader patterns of human–AI interaction in society. I examine AI as a socially situated technology whose design reflects broader societal values. I argue that AI is non-neutral not only because it embeds these values, but because it reshapes the affective mechanisms through which people judge, feel, and decide. To capture this dynamic, I use a Human–AI–Society framework that highlights a feedback loop: societal norms shape AI systems; AI mediates cognition and affect; and these micro-level shifts accumulate to influence institutional practices and cultural trajectories. This perspective situates my empirical work on AI-mediation within a broader inquiry into how technologies reconfigure the affective conditions of human judgment in contemporary society.
